02The Frame

Your junior is using Claude shamelessly. How do you know which of them can do the job?

By the time the candidate reaches your inbox, Claude has written the CV, shipped the take-home, deployed the portfolio site, and rehearsed the answers for the final round. Each artefact you once used to judge competence now arrives polished from the same model, and nothing left in the funnel tells you which of the candidates in front of you could ship anything without it.

Hiring needs to watch the candidate work with the model in the room.

04The CV

A recruiter spends six seconds on a CV.

Six seconds is what a recruiter spends on a CV before deciding whether to triage it further, and that window is enough to read a school name, a couple of company logos, and three or four keywords from the role on file, but far too short to learn anything about how the candidate thinks or what they could ship on a Tuesday.

A CV tells an employer what the candidate sat through, and none of it is the work itself.
05The Take-Home

Take-homes started leaking the moment Claude could finish them in twenty minutes.

For a long time the take-home was the closest hiring came to watching the candidate do real work, because they had to sit with a problem for a few hours and ship something a hiring manager could read end to end. Then frontier models made it cheap to ship a clean PR overnight without weighing a single trade-off along the way, and the artefact has carried no signal since.

01 · Authorship
No way to know who wrote the code.
A submitted PR looks identical whether the candidate sat with it for four hours or piped the brief into Cursor and went to bed.
02 · Effort
Twenty minutes and twenty hours read the same.
The submission is a frozen artefact. The path the candidate took to reach it, the trade-offs they weighed, the things they tried and dropped, all lost.
03 · Comparison
No two take-homes can be ranked.
Each candidate gets a slightly different brief, with a different scope and a different bar, so the hiring manager forms an impression they have no shared rubric to check against.
Hiring managers know this, and they keep shipping the take-home because they have nothing better.
06The Portfolio

Anyone can ship a portfolio in five minutes, so the polish stopped meaning anything about the person behind it.

A portfolio was once the proof a CV could not give, because shipping one took weeks and the result told the hiring manager something about how the candidate worked when nobody was watching. The bar for putting a clean, deployed site up dropped to a Cursor prompt and a Vercel deploy, and the polish on the page stopped being a signal about the person behind it.

07What They Want

What hiring managers want is to watch the candidate do the work.

What they want, in detail, is the artefact a candidate would have built in their first month on the job, and the reasoning behind the moves that produced it. Three properties make that readable on a hiring manager's desk.

01 · Real
Pulled from the job the candidate would do on day one.
The task is one of the things the candidate would ship in their first month if hired, so reading the submission tells the employer about the role itself.
02 · Comparable
Same brief, same rubric, ranked.
All applicants get the same project for the same role, scored on the same rubric, and the hiring manager opens a ranked stack with the rubric behind each card.
03 · Time-bound
The clock turns the artefact back into a signal.
A visible window makes the submission a record of what the candidate can produce in a stretch of time. The clock is what makes the work mean something again.
A founder whiteboarding the three features the team needs this quarter already knows the shape of the test they would put a hire through, and no way to run it on a stranger.
08The Reframe

What if the application was the work?

If a candidate shipped a piece of the actual job before they applied, you would see who was thinking with the model and who was just letting it ship, and decide whether to interview on the strength of what they had built.

Both sides start the job before either side commits to it.
09The Product

Candela v8: hiring rebuilt around the work itself.

A founder pastes the role they are hiring for, and Candela writes the project a strong candidate would have to ship to prove they could do the job from week one. The candidate finds the role through the company's page, takes the project on a visible window, and builds it inside the existing workspace with the thinking partner beside them. The submission is scored against the rubric the role generated, and the hiring manager opens the work that cleared the bar and reads it before deciding whether to call.

01 · Job-as-Project
Any JD becomes a real project the candidate can build.
On paste, Candela reads the role and writes the project a strong candidate would ship to prove they can do it from week one, with the milestones, the starter scaffolding, and the rubric ready underneath.
02 · Live Build
Candidates build inside Candela, on a visible clock.
The candidate forks the project into the existing Candela workspace and builds with the thinking partner beside them, the reasoning log captures how they think, and the clock turns the artefact back into a signal.
03 · AI Shortlist
Submissions are scored against the rubric the JD generated.
When the candidate ships, the work and the reasoning log freeze and get scored against the same five-criterion rubric. The hiring manager opens a ranked pipeline and reads only the top of the stack.

12The Brief

What Candela writes is the project itself.

On paste, the role becomes a structured brief with three milestones in sequence, a repo skeleton and a sample input the candidate can clone from the next click, and a five-criterion rubric pulled from the role and weighted to it. Each applicant for that role is scored against the same rubric.

01 · Milestones
Three slices, in sequence.
The first slice proves the candidate can stand the project up, the second exposes the trade-off the role will face on the job, and the third tests how they ship it for the next hire.
02 · Scaffolding
A repo skeleton, a sample input, a README stub.
The candidate starts from the same place the role would start from. The first commit lands on the project itself, with the boilerplate already sitting in the scaffold.
03 · Rubric
Five criteria, weighted from the JD.
Reasoning depth, scope discipline, code quality, communication, and iteration honesty, weighted by the role on file, with the same rubric scoring each applicant for that role.
The hiring manager wrote a job description, and Candela shipped a comparable, time-bound, real project on top of it.

17Submit
